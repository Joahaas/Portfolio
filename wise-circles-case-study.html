Description

This project was developed in response to the D&AD New Blood Awards 2025 brief set by Wise (formerly TransferWise), a global financial technology company that helps people and businesses move money internationally without hidden fees. The brief asked designers to think beyond Wise's current offerings and create a solution that aligns with the brand's values of transparency, accessibility, and user empowerment. It was an open invitation to explore how Wise could become even more relevant in the daily lives of a younger, global audience.

Target audience

Wise gave us options to choose between 4 of their user segments - Travelers, Global Networkers, Nomads & Relocators. We chose the Travelers: These users primarily reside in their home country but frequently travel internationally, often for leisure or short-term stays.

Research

BEFORE WE SOLVED, WE LISTENED

We did a Desk Research and Field Research with surveys and interviews to users to understand their pains and behavioral patterns when using WISE.

Desk Research

Firstly we studied the current flow of the wise app to understand for ourselves how the app was structured and how the information architecture worked inside the app.

We also studied its design system to understand its brand, purpose and overall voice of the business.

After that we did a competitor analysis to understand what were its users doing differently than wise not only for the segment we decided to go for but also for other users.

We looked at:

Revolut

We looked at Revolut's bill-splitting and group features to understand how they manage shared payments within a sleek, modern interface. While the experience is smooth, we saw room for improvement in how group interactions and reminders are handled.

PayPal

PayPal's peer-to-peer features gave us insight into trusted, secure transactions ‚Äî but the UX felt outdated and not optimized for casual, social use. This helped us focus on keeping things simple, transparent, and human in Wise Circles.

Venmo

Venmo stood out for its social, casual approach to money between friends. We took note of its friendly tone and lightweight experience, while thinking critically about privacy and how to make the experience feel more relevant for international users.

N26

We looked at N26 for inspiration on clean, minimal design and user flow. While their interface is polished, we felt their group payment features were limited, so we explored ways to offer more flexibility and clarity when splitting costs.

MBWAY

As a Portuguese solution, MB Way helped us understand how users in our local context manage quick payments and split bills. However, it lacks strong group features or smart organization, which we aimed to improve on in our concept.

Then we took a look at different articles, blogs, reddit trends, WISE annual reports and others different sources of information that could be valuable in this research phase.

Field Research

In this phase we made 2 forms one for daily - avid users of WISE, and we found necessary or it would be insightful if we did another one that was only for our segment of users the - travelers.

WISE Users survey: Key Insights

Most users interact with Wise daily, mainly for sending money to family or making international payments.

60% still split expenses manually, showing a gap for built-in group money tools.

Users rated the interface as intuitive, but suggested improvements in clarity, speed, and fee transparency.

There's clear demand for a simpler way to track and split shared expenses inside the app.

Travelers survey: Key Insights

Most are digital nomads or frequent travelers, often on the move and sharing costs with friends or partners.

Manual reimbursement is still the default ‚Äî even though it causes friction like people forgetting to pay back.

Top concerns include exchange rates, fees, and tracking shared expenses fairly.

Few use actual split-bill tools, but many expressed a need for one, especially during group travel.

Insights & Opportunities

REAL PROBLEMS = REAL DESIGN FUEL

Main Pain points we found in our research:

Confusion Over Splits

Who paid for what and how much does each person owe? Manually keeping track leads to errors, frustration, or mistrust.

Potential Group Tension

Money misunderstandings can put a damper on the trip's mood, breed resentment, or cause awkwardness in the group dynamic.

Currency confusion

Converting amounts for every transaction is cumbersome. Hidden fees or poor rates create tension over fairness (e.g., if one friend ends up paying more due to bank charges).

Time-Consuming Logistics

Constant calculations, note-taking, or back-and-forth chat messages. Can distract from actually enjoying the trip.

Payment Inconsistencies

Different travelers use different banks, each with its own fees and exchange rates, making cost splitting even more complicated.

End-of-Trip Stress

Large outstanding balances often get settled at the last minute, or even after returning home, causing ongoing stress and potential resentment.

What were the opportunities for innovation?

Our research revealed that while Wise excels at individual international transfers, it lacks native support for group-based financial interactions‚Äî a need that's increasingly common among younger users who travel, split housing, or share experiences.

This opened several key opportunities for innovation:

Enable seamless group expense management

Users currently rely on multiple apps (WhatsApp, Splitwise, spreadsheets) to track and settle group expenses. There's a clear opportunity to bring this functionality into Wise natively, reducing friction and enhancing convenience.

Make shared money more social, transparent, and less awkward

Managing money with friends or groups often leads to tension. By designing a feature that adds clarity, reminders, and visual summaries, we can reduce stress and improve user trust.

Create a daily-use habit around Wise

Right now, Wise is used mostly for one-off transfers. A group feature can shift Wise into the space of everyday financial coordination, increasing engagement and retention.

Position Wise as the go-to solution for digital nomads and group travelers

A feature that supports shared costs while traveling internationally would give Wise a unique edge among its competitors.

We did a brainstorming section and each member did a 11 star experience exercise ü§ì

1

Digital Dust-Up

Imagine a digital version of scattered receipts. Everyone's using different apps or spreadsheets, or maybe just notes on their phone. The end of the trip involves a frantic exchange of screenshots, messages, and calculations, often leading to confusion and frustration.

2

Shared Spreadsheet Struggle

One person is designated the "spreadsheet master," trying to keep track of everything. It's a shared document, but it's still a chore to update, and there's always the nagging feeling that something's been missed.

5

Seamless Sync

The Wise app becomes a central hub. Creating a group is easy, logging expenses is quick and intuitive, and everyone can see the running total. It's like a shared digital wallet, transparent and convenient.

6

Helpful Hint

The app starts to anticipate needs. It suggests expense categories, reminds people to log their contributions, and offers personalized budgeting tips based on past trips. It's like having a helpful travel companion.

7

Smart Assistant

The app becomes more proactive. It suggests splitting options based on the type of expense, offers real-time currency conversion, and even helps find deals and discounts. It's like having a financial travel agent in your pocket.

10

Effortless Flow

The app integrates seamlessly with other travel platforms. Shared expenses are automatically logged, currency conversions are handled behind the scenes, and settlements are instant and frictionless. It's like the financial side of the trip just takes care of itself.

11

Invisible Hand

The app fades into the background, becoming almost invisible. It anticipates needs before they arise, suggests budget-friendly options, and even negotiates group discounts. At the end of the trip, it generates a personalized travelogue and automatically settles balances, accounting for any agreed-upon variations.

What I Learned

ME, BUT WISER! üòå

What did I learn from this project about design, teamwork, or users?

This project taught me that designing for real people means designing for real tension ‚Äî especially when it comes to money. Group expenses are messy not just because of numbers, but because of emotions, habits, and unspoken rules. Understanding that helped me think more empathetically, not just functionally. It taught me how it can be fun and exciting working in the fintech industry maybe in the future.

On the teamwork side, I learned how valuable it is to lean into different strengths within the group. We moved fast, split responsibilities fluidly, and tried to keep adapting. It reminded me that good design is collaborative by nature ‚Äî not something you do alone in a vacuum.

What would I do differently next time?

Next time, I'd test the idea first we didn't have much time to do it. I also loved some other ideas that we had I would have loved to experiment a little bit more with those before settling on one specific direction.
